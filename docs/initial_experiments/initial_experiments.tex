\section{Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

Context $c_t = s_t.\texttt{activity} \in \{\texttt{sedentary}, \texttt{active}\}$. Each algorithm maintains separate parameters per $(c, a)$ pair for the contextual variants:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Thompson Sampling:} $(\alpha_{c,a}, \beta_{c,a})$ posteriors. Sample $\theta_{c_t, a} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_{c_t, a}, \beta_{c_t, a})$, pick $a^* = \arg\max_a \theta_{c_t, a}$.
  \item \textbf{Epsilon-Greedy:} $Q_{c,a}$ values. With probability $\epsilon$ explore, otherwise $a^* = \arg\max_a Q_{c_t, a}$.
  \item \textbf{UCB:} $a^* = \arg\max_a \left[ Q_{c_t, a} + c \sqrt{\ln(N_{c_t}) / n_{c_t, a}} \right]$ where $N_{c_t} = \sum_a n_{c_t, a}$.
  \item \textbf{Decaying Epsilon-Greedy:} Linear decay: $\epsilon_t = \max(\epsilon_{\min},\; \epsilon_{\text{start}} (1 - t / T_{\text{decay}}))$.
\end{itemize}

Non-contextual variants collapse $c$ and maintain a single set of $(\alpha_a, \beta_a)$, $Q_a$, or $n_a$.

\section{Action Space Extension}
\label{sec:action}

References \texttt{docs/design/initial\_design.tex \S3} and Issue \#126.

\subsection{ActionConfig}
\label{sec:action-config}
The action space is extended from 2 to 6 actions, each with a \texttt{burden\_penalty} representing the cost of sending that type of message. The full set:

Action & Description & Burden Penalty \\
\midrule

\texttt{progress\_feedback} & Show step-count progress & 0.10 \\
\texttt{goal\_reminder} & Remind of daily goal & 0.15 \\
\texttt{motivational\_prompt} & Generic encouragement & 0.20 \\
\texttt{recovery\_suggestion} & Suggest recovery activity & 0.25 \\

Burden penalties are stored as metadata in the config via \texttt{ActionConfig(name, burden\_penalty)} but are not yet wired into the reward function. The \texttt{action\_names} property on \texttt{MDPConfig} normalises either format (\texttt{list[str]} or \texttt{list[ActionConfig]}) to plain string lists for agent consumption.

\subsection{Transition Model Updates}

The transition matrix grows from $2\times2\times2$ to $6\times2\times2$ (6 actions, 2 states, 2 next states). The design creates a contextual gap: the optimal action differs by state.

\begin{align*}
P_{\text{no\_message}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.90 & 0.10 \\
0.15 & 0.85
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{progress\_feedback}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.75 & 0.25 \\
0.20 & 0.80
\end{bmatrix}
\\
P_{\text{goal\_reminder}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.80 & 0.20 \\
0.35 & 0.65
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{motivational\_prompt}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.55 & 0.45 \\
0.40 & 0.60
\end{bmatrix}
\\
P_{\text{recovery\_suggestion}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.65 & 0.35 \\
0.45 & 0.55
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{walking\_suggestion}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.70 & 0.30 \\
0.30 & 0.70
\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}

\noindent Columns are $\{\text{sedentary}, \text{active}\}$. For sedentary users, \texttt{motivational\_prompt} ($0.45 \to \text{active}$) is most effective; for active users, \texttt{no\_message} ($0.85 \to \text{active}$) is best --- sending any message to an already-active user risks backfiring (Reactance theory). This asymmetry creates a gap between contextual and non-contextual optimal policies.

\subsection{Agent Compatibility}
\label{sec:agent-compat}

All four bandit agents handle variable-length action sets without modification. The \texttt{action\_names} property extracts plain strings from \texttt{ActionConfig} dicts, so agents continue to receive \texttt{list[str]}. No agent-side changes were required.

\subsection{Open Questions}
Burden penalties remain metadata-only; wiring them into the reward function is deferred to a follow-up experiment.

\section{Combined Results}
\label{sec:results}

End-to-end evaluation on \texttt{rule\_based\_with\_actions.yaml}: 50 random seeds, 450 timesteps each. Results use the best hyperparameters found per agent variant (see \S\ref{sec:hyperparam}).

\subsection{Optimal Reward}

The optimal contextual policy is state-dependent:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{When sedentary:} choose \texttt{motivational\_prompt} ($P(\to\text{active}) = 0.45$)
  \item \textbf{When active:} choose \texttt{no\_message} ($P(\to\text{active}) = 0.85$)
\end{itemize}

Stationary distribution under this policy: $\pi_{\text{active}} = \frac{3}{4}$, $\pi_{\text{sedentary}} = \frac{1}{4}$. Expected per-step contextual reward:
\[
E[r_{\text{ctx}}] = 1.0 \cdot \frac{3}{4} + 0.0 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = 0.75
\]
Total contextual optimal: $0.75 \times 450 = 337.5$.

The optimal non-contextual policy picks the single best action across both states: \texttt{progress\_feedback} ($P(\to\text{active}\mid\text{sed})=0.25$, $P(\to\text{active}\mid\text{act})=0.80$). Stationary distribution: $\pi_{\text{active}} = \frac{5}{9}$. Expected per-step non-contextual reward:
\[
E[r_{\text{nctx}}] = \frac{5}{9} \approx 0.556
\]
Total non-contextual optimal: $\frac{5}{9} \times 450 = 250.0$.

The contextual policy achieves $34.9\%$ higher per-step reward ($0.75$ vs $0.556$).

\subsection{Learning Curves}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/learning_curves.pdf}
\caption{Cumulative and per-step reward (50 seeds, $\pm$1~SD). Contextual agents converge toward the $0.75$ bound; standard agents plateau near $0.556$. Both optimal lines are shown --- they differ because the best action depends on the current state.}
\label{fig:learning-curves}
\end{figure}

Contextual agents outperform standard agents across all four algorithms, driven by the state-dependent optimal policy gap ($0.75$ vs $0.556$). Contextual UCB at $c=0.2$ achieves the best overall result at $88.7\%$ of the contextual optimal. DEC's $\epsilon$ decay provides effective early exploration while exploiting later. Standard agents hover within $8$--$10\%$ of their non-contextual $0.556$ bound, confirming that the gap is structural (state-dependent optimal action) rather than a learning deficiency.

\subsection{State Space and Reward Extensions}
Enriched state features (archetype, burden, goal progress) and multi-timescale reward (immediate + 3-week delayed clinical outcome) are deferred to follow-up work. These require corresponding extensions to the agent architecture (full RL rather than bandit) and are tracked in Issues \#124 and \#132.
