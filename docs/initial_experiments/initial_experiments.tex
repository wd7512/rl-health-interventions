\subsection{ActionConfig}

\label{sec:action-config}
The action space is extended from 2 to 6 actions, each with a \texttt{burden\_penalty} representing the cost of sending that type of message. The full set:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Action & Description & Burden Penalty \\
\midrule
\texttt{no\_message} & Do nothing & 0.00 \\
\texttt{progress\_feedback} & Show step-count progress & 0.10 \\
\texttt{goal\_reminder} & Remind of daily goal & 0.15 \\
\texttt{motivational\_prompt} & Generic encouragement & 0.20 \\
\texttt{recovery\_suggestion} & Suggest recovery activity & 0.25 \\
\texttt{walking\_suggestion} & Suggest a walk & 0.30 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{The six actions and their burden penalties. Higher burden actions are expected to be more effective but risk user disengagement.}
\label{tab:actions}
\end{table}

Burden penalties are stored as metadata in the config via \texttt{ActionConfig(name, burden\_penalty)} but are not yet wired into the reward function. The \texttt{action\_names} property on \texttt{MDPConfig} normalises either format (\texttt{list[str]} or \texttt{list[ActionConfig]}) to plain string lists for agent consumption.


\subsection{Transition Model Updates}

The transition matrix grows from $2\times2\times2$ to $6\times2\times2$ (6 actions, 2 states, 2 next states). Each action defines a row:

\begin{align*}
P_{\text{no\_message}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.9 & 0.1 \\
0.4 & 0.6
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{progress\_feedback}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.75 & 0.25 \\
0.3 & 0.7
\end{bmatrix}
\\
P_{\text{goal\_reminder}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.8 & 0.2 \\
0.35 & 0.65
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{motivational\_prompt}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.7 & 0.3 \\
0.3 & 0.7
\end{bmatrix}
\\
P_{\text{recovery\_suggestion}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.65 & 0.35 \\
0.25 & 0.75
\end{bmatrix}
&
P_{\text{walking\_suggestion}} &=
\begin{bmatrix}
0.6 & 0.4 \\
0.2 & 0.8
\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}

\noindent Columns are $\{\text{sedentary}, \text{active}\}$. \texttt{walking\_suggestion} has the strongest effect in both states, making it the optimal action everywhere. Backward compatibility: \texttt{ActionConfig} dicts are accepted alongside plain strings via the updated schema validator.


\subsection{Agent Compatibility}
\label{sec:agent-compat}

All four bandit agents (Thompson Sampling, $\epsilon$-Greedy, UCB1, Decaying $\epsilon$-Greedy) handle variable-length action sets without modification. The \texttt{action\_names} property extracts plain strings from \texttt{ActionConfig} dicts, so agents continue to receive \texttt{list[str]}. No agent-side changes were required.

\section{Combined Results}
\label{sec:results}

End-to-end evaluation on \texttt{rule\_based\_with\_actions.yaml}: 50 random seeds, 450 timesteps each. Results use the best hyperparameters found per agent variant (see \S\ref{sec:hyperparam}).

\subsection{Optimal Reward}

Under the 6-action MDP, the optimal policy always selects \texttt{walking\_suggestion}, which maximises $P(\text{active})$ from both states:

\[
P(\text{active} \mid \text{sedentary}, \text{walking}) = 0.4, \qquad
P(\text{active} \mid \text{active}, \text{walking}) = 0.8
\]

Stationary distribution: $\pi_{\text{active}} = \frac{2}{3}$, $\pi_{\text{sedentary}} = \frac{1}{3}$. Expected per-step reward:

\[
E[r] = 1.0 \cdot \frac{2}{3} + 0.0 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.6667
\]

Total optimal reward: $\frac{2}{3} \times 450 = 300$. Because the same action is optimal in both states, the contextual and non-contextual bounds coincide.

\subsection{Learning Curves}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/learning_curves.pdf}
\caption{Cumulative and per-step reward (50 seeds, $\pm$1~SD). Standard DEC and UCB converge fastest; contextual variants lag due to data fragmentation across 12 context--action pairs.}
\label{fig:learning-curves}
\end{figure}

Standard variants outperform contextual ones across all four algorithms, contrary to the 2-action MVP results. With 6 actions, the $\frac{2}{3}$ optimal bound is reachable without state conditioning (the same action is best in both states), so contextualisation adds data fragmentation without benefit. DEC's $\epsilon$ decay provides effective exploration early while exploiting later, yielding the best overall result at $91.2\%$ of optimal.
