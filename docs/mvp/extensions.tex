Extends the MVP bandits (\S5 of \texttt{mvp\_specification.tex}) by conditioning action selection on the current activity level, and adds a decaying-epsilon variant with automated hyperparameter selection.

\section{Formulation}
Context $c_t = s_t.\texttt{activity} \in \{\texttt{sedentary}, \texttt{active}\}$. Each algorithm maintains separate parameters per $(c, a)$ pair instead of global per-action parameters:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Thompson Sampling:} $(\alpha_{c,a}, \beta_{c,a})$ posteriors. Sample $\theta_{c_t, a} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_{c_t, a}, \beta_{c_t, a})$, pick $a^* = \arg\max_a \theta_{c_t, a}$.
  \item \textbf{Epsilon-Greedy:} $Q_{c,a}$ values. With probability $\epsilon$ explore, otherwise pick $a^* = \arg\max_a Q_{c_t, a}$.
  \item \textbf{UCB:} $Q_{c,a}$ values with per-context counts. $a^* = \arg\max_a \left[ Q_{c_t, a} + c \sqrt{\ln(N_{c_t}) / n_{c_t, a}} \right]$ where $N_{c_t} = \sum_a n_{c_t, a}$ is the total visits to the current context.
  \item \textbf{Decaying Epsilon-Greedy:} Same Q-learning as epsilon-greedy, but the exploration rate decays linearly each step: $\epsilon_t = \max(\epsilon_{\min},\; \epsilon_{\text{start}} (1 - t / T_{\text{decay}}))$. After $T_{\text{decay}}$ steps the agent relies almost entirely on exploitation (subject to $\epsilon_{\min}$).
\end{itemize}

Update only the observed $(c_t, a_t)$ pair.

\section{Optimal Policy}
The optimal contextual policy (from the transition matrices in \S3) is: nudge when sedentary ($0.3 > 0.1$), idle when active ($0.6 > 0.5$). Stationary distribution: $\pi_s = 4/7$, $\pi_a = 3/7$.

\[
E[r] = \tfrac{4}{7} \cdot 0.3 + \tfrac{3}{7} \cdot 0.6 = \tfrac{3}{7} \approx 0.429 \text{ per step} \implies 192.9 \text{ total}
\]

Best non-contextual (always nudge): $E[r] = 0.375$, total $168.8$. The contextual bound is $14.3\%$ higher.

The widened grid fully resolves all boundary concerns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{EG:} The optimum ($\epsilon=0.15$, total 160.7) is interior --- values on either side ($0.10$, $0.20$) are both lower. Not at a boundary.
  \item \textbf{UCB:} The optimum ($c=0.5$, total 163.6) is interior --- $c=0.3$ yields 163.5 and $c=0.7$ yields 159.8. Not at a boundary.
  \item \textbf{DEC (standard):} The new optimum ($\epsilon_{\text{start}}=0.6$, $T_{\text{decay}}=150$, total 163.9) is interior --- the grid now extends to $\epsilon_{\text{start}}=0.8$ and $T_{\text{decay}}=500$, with both directions showing diminishing returns. Gains above 162.0 are modest.
  \item \textbf{DEC (contextual):} The new optimum ($\epsilon_{\text{start}}=0.7$, $T_{\text{decay}}=75$, total 180.4) is also interior, with $\epsilon_{\text{start}}=0.8$ and longer/shorter decay windows all yielding lower totals.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Variance: $\epsilon=0.05$ vs $\epsilon=0.10$ for EG.}
Comparing the two values from the original grid: $\epsilon=0.05$ achieves total reward 158.1 $\pm$ 19.9 with last-50 mean 0.375; $\epsilon=0.10$ achieves 158.1 $\pm$ 17.8 with last-50 mean 0.370. The means are nearly identical, but $\epsilon=0.10$ has $\sim$11\% lower standard deviation. For deployment, $\epsilon=0.10$ offers a more consistent policy with equivalent cumulative reward, while $\epsilon=0.05$ extracts marginally better final-step performance at the cost of higher variance.

With optimised hyperparameters, contextual TS and contextual UCB both reach the per-context optimal reward by the last 50 steps ($0.429$ and $0.430$, within noise of the theoretical $3/7$). Contextual EG changes from $-11.0\%$ (with $\epsilon=0.1$) to $-13.6\%$ (with $\epsilon=0.05$) --- a small regression driven by higher variance --- while contextual DEC ($-13.4\%$) performs comparably. Standard UCB benefits most from the hyperparameter search, jumping from $-23.1\%$ (with $c=2.0$) to $-15.2\%$ (with $c=0.5$), outperforming standard TS and EG.

The decaying-epsilon agent achieves slightly higher total reward than its fixed-epsilon counterpart in the non-contextual setting ($161.7$ vs $158.1$) by front-loading exploration. In the contextual setting the advantage disappears, likely because the per-context Q-values already provide sufficient exploration through their slower convergence.
