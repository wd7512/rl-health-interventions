## Executive Summary

This scan catalogues 25 papers (2020-2026) on just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs) across a wide range of health domains. The primary focus domains are smoking cessation (the most active JITAI domain outside of physical activity), followed by substance use, mental health, sleep, weight management, gambling, HIV medication adherence, and workplace stress. The scan deliberately includes negative/null results, engagement challenges, and user burden discussions.

**Key takeaway:** The JITAI field is growing rapidly but faces persistent challenges around engagement decay, the engagement paradox, sensor/EMA burden, and a gap between academic JITAI research and commercial app implementation.

## Negative / Null Results Summary

**8 papers with explicit negative or null findings:**

1. **Hebert 2020** — Smart-T2 JITAI vs QuitGuide vs usual care: NO significant difference in abstinence (22% vs 15% vs 15%, P>.05)
2. **Perski 2022** — Systematic review: effectiveness evidence was mixed across 14 substance use JITAI studies
3. **Teepe 2021** — None of 28 top depression apps implemented JITAI mechanisms; no publications evaluated JITAI in apps
4. **Nahum-Shani 2026** — Flags the engagement paradox as a fundamental unsolved challenge
5. **Dorsch 2025** — myBPmyLife RCT (n=602): null primary outcome (SBP -5.2 vs -5.7, p=0.76)
6. **Yang 2023** — Mixed feasibility: only 63% met sensor wearing benchmark
7. **Vinci 2025** — Proximal effects only significant in Week 2, not Week 1
8. **Suh 2024** — Subgroups (women, high neuroticism) less likely to benefit from workplace stress JITAI

## Cross-Domain Patterns and Insights

### Engagement Decay
- Consistently reported across domains, especially with high-burden EMA protocols
- Compliance ranges from 15% to 95% depending on domain and protocol
- Workplace context adds unique constraints (meetings, focus time)

### User Burden
- Wearable sensors significantly increase burden (only 63% met wearing benchmarks in Yang 2023)
- Multiple EMAs (5x/day for 5 weeks) may contribute to null results through participant fatigue
- Passive sensing and context-aware triggers are promising solutions but not yet mature

### The Engagement Paradox
- Nahum-Shani & Murphy (2026) identify this as Challenge #1: individuals may not be able to engage when they need the intervention most
- No paper in this scan offers a compelling solution to this paradox

### Methodological Issues
- Most JITAIs still use **static (time-invariant) decision rules** rather than dynamic ML-based rules
- 31.4% of weight management JITAIs used ML (Koh 2025) — a minority
- Terminology inconsistency: many studies use the JITAI label loosely without implementing its core components
- Few studies report development costs, infrastructure, or implementation feasibility

### Gap Between Research and Practice
- Teepe et al. (2021) starkly demonstrated that top commercial depression apps do NOT use JITAI mechanisms
- Academic JITAI research is not translating to consumer-facing health apps

## Implications for RL-Driven JITAIs

1. **Null results are common** — JITAI RCTs often fail to show significant primary outcomes. This underscores the need for rigorous evaluation of any RL-based JITAI, especially if the RL component adds complexity without clear benefit.

2. **Engagement is the binding constraint** — Even well-designed JITAIs face engagement decay. An RL policy that optimizes for proximal outcomes may inadvertently increase burden and drive disengagement. The RL system must model the engagement cost of each intervention.

3. **The engagement paradox is unsolved** — No existing approach reliably addresses the fact that users need help most when they are least able to engage. This is a first-order challenge for any JITAI, including RL-based ones.

4. **Passive sensing reduces burden but is not a panacea** — Sensor non-adherence (63% benchmark in Yang 2023) shows that even "passive" sensors impose real burden.

5. **Context matters** — The Suh et al. (2024) finding that rescheduled interventions have higher engagement (OR 1.77) suggests that RL policies should incorporate user state and context (not just clinical need).

6. **Reporting standards needed** — The field lacks harmonized reporting, making it difficult to compare RL-based JITAIs against traditional rule-based ones.

7. **Gap between research and practice** — Commercial health apps rarely implement JITAI mechanisms, suggesting that even demonstrably effective RL policies may face adoption barriers.
